Web Design
Sour/Dough

Sour/Dough | Web Design

Project Description

Established in 2016, Sour/Dough is the epitome of all things breakfast and lunch. Their goal is to provide a brunch menu using the freshest, local, and organic ingredients. It was important to understand the owner's vision for this brunch place. After multiple iterations, I came up with this minimal yet effective logo design.

Initial Designs

The initial sketch was designed based on the requirements and the design goals. It was important to perform card sorting sessions to determine the menu. We also had to perform a series of brainstorming sessions and user research to determine the content and the flow of information. These set of activities gave me a design plan, thoughts for refinement, and understand the limitations.

Low-fidelity

Based on the design solution, the low fidelity prototype was created. It was important to get user feedback on these prototypes. A huge portion of these surveys was conducted via e-mail with the lo-fi prototype sent as an attachment. Conducting and analyzing the results of the two sessions of surveys (one before the feedback and one after) was a challenging task. Our objective during this phase was to develop a detailed understanding of the target users to help us focus on their needs.


Design and Visuals

Having conducted competitive analysis as well as planning out the overall design language, I came up with the high-fidelity model for Sour/Dough's landing page. The sections are designed to match with the shape of their logo.

Finally, we landed at the last period of our venture, the Usability Testing. We had to make arrangements for the pre-test and post-test poll to get data, for example, ease of completing a task and their assessments about Sour/Dough's website.

Heuristic Analysis
Disney Website

Disney Website | Heuristics

Project Description

On a relaxed Sunday afternoon, while browsing the internet, I bumped into Disney’s website. My first thought after looking at Disney’s website was that why does it contains so many large images and texts on a homepage?

One would think that this website is specifically designed for kids. However, in my opinion, Disney needs to broaden its demographic range. Even though their largest age group range is among kids, realistically users of all ages browse through Disney’s website for some or the other reason. Be it shopping a Disney product or finding information about a Disney movie or planning a trip to Disneyland.

After browsing through the website, I decided to do a quick heuristic evaluation.

Major Issues

Unappealing visuals:

The site fails to have a consistent and clear look to it. The size of the header and footer are not consistently styled as we browse through the sub-pages. Not having a recognizable look might make not help in keeping the users engaged.


With the amount of visuals present on a single page, there are high chances that the user might get overwhelmed about what to do next and might simply close the browser.


Visual momentum:

While browsing through different sections, I realized that it takes multiple clicks to return to the homepage. This spoils the whole user experience as well as might confuse the user. For example, when on the games.disney.com page, one would expect that clicking on the Disney logo would return them to the homepage. However, this is not the case. Clicking on the Disney logo opens up a large menu which contains the hyperlink for the homepage along with a list of hyperlinks for other pages on the website.

Closing the large grey menu might lead to pyschological gap because there is no clear exit button displayed (the Disney logo with the minimizing arrow is not visible enough). Users will have to find out a way to close the menu (in this case, by clicking on the blank space on the website).


Context and layout:

The images used on the websites are very inconsistent in terms of styling and alignment. For example, some images sharp corners while others have rounded corners. Some of images used on the website seem to be of low quality and pixelated.

Also, due to the high amount of images and videos on the homepage, it takes a long time to load. Another issue with the home page is that the layout doesn’t help the user to focus their attention on what to do next.


Lack of interaction:

While hovering the cursor over those large images and menus, you expect some type of animation, change of text color or at least some useful information to be displayed. This helps the user understand that those images are dynamic and clickable. This type of interaction is missing and makes the users feel that those are just static images. Items that aren't clickable do not have characteristics they should possess. Thus, lowering the click rate.


Mixed scrolling:

As you scroll through the Disney’s homepage, you will find a lot of images and information cluttered in one page. There is high amount of vertical and horizontal scrolling which would distract the user from their original goal as it consists data about the submenus. For a user with poor spatial visualization skill, this type of scrolling might be challenging. Using horizontal scrolling on non-touch devices might be painful for the users.


Zooming of the widgets:

I tried to zoom out to 30% of the orignal size and found out that not all the widgets of the webpage change their sizes. Some of the widgets such as the main slider and featured videos remain at the original size.


Recommendations

Reduce the amount of scrolling. This will encourage user confidence through consistent navigation. The user must be able to navigate any area of the site from all pages of the site.

Provide consistency in links, images, and layout.

Keep information on one domain. This will make it easy for maintenance as well as cost effective. For example, the Disney Store webpage opens in a completely different domain.

Avoid unnecessary interactions. This causes extra work in developing and maintaining the website for the web developers which his completely unnecessary. It will also simply the overall user experience.

Branding
ISRC

Interactive Systems Research Center (ISRC) | Branding

Project Description

This was a HCC 710 coursework project on branding. The client Interactive Systems Research Center (ISRC) wanted to be recognized as an independent brand at UMBC. This project involved multiple critique sessions which helped in understanding perspective of the client and the users in a more refined manner. The design and color choice of the logo is based upon the ISRC mission statement, "ISRC acts as a bridge to industry, community, and organizations in order to foster long-standing relationships in addressing user needs through interactive computing solutions."

The set of lines present on both side of the 'Interactive Systems Research Center' wordmark represents the users needs and the solution. However, there is a good amount of gap between these two sets. This gap is bridged by ISRC. Also, the 3 colors used in this logo represents the industry, community, and organizations.

Design and Visuals

From this assignment I learned that is important to maintain a design language while designing for a brand. Since it would allow different components a good amount of resemblance under a single entity.

Components involved in ISRC branding were letterhead, folder, half flyer, business card, CD, and stationaries.
